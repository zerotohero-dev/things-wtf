§01Why bother with these?

Throw a ball. Any ball, any direction. Watch its path. It rises, slows, stops climbing, and falls back down — and the shape it traces through the air is a parabola. Not a triangle, not a circle, not a line. A parabola, every single time.

Point a satellite dish at the sky: the bowl is a parabola, because it focuses every incoming radio wave onto a single point. Look inside a car's headlight: parabola, because it sends every ray of light from one bulb out as parallel beams. Push water through a garden hose held at an angle: parabola. Drop a marble down a spinning bucket: the surface of the water forms a parabola.

The shape comes from a single, modest-looking equation. Once you can read it, you can predict where the ball lands, how tall the bridge needs to be, when the rocket reaches peak altitude, where to place the antenna at the focus of a dish. That equation — the one that draws all these curves — is a quadratic, or as your textbook will phrase it, a second-degree equation.

WHAT YOU'LL GET FROM THIS PAGE

By the end, you will be able to look at any quadratic equation and (a) know what its graph looks like before drawing it, (b) find its highest or lowest point, (c) find where it crosses the x-axis if it does, and (d) re-write it in whichever form you need. Each piece will be color-coded so you don't have to track which letter is which while you're also doing arithmetic.

§02What "second degree" actually means

The degree of an equation is just the highest power of $x$ that appears in it. Nothing fancier than that.

1st degree (LINEAR)

Example$\;3x + 7 = 0$

Highest power of $x$ is $1$. The graph is a straight line.

2nd degree (QUADRATIC)

Example$\;2x^2 + 3x + 7 = 0$

Highest power of $x$ is $2$. The graph is a parabola.

3rd degree (CUBIC)

Example$\;x^3 - x = 0$

Highest power is $3$. The graph is a curvy "S" shape.

So "second-degree equation" is just a longer way of saying "an equation where the biggest power of $x$ is $2$." Wherever you see $x^2$ as the largest term, you're holding a quadratic.

HEADS-UP

The $x^2$ term must actually be there — and its coefficient (the number in front of it) cannot be zero. If it were zero, the $x^2$ would vanish and you'd be left with a plain linear equation. We'll see why that matters in a second.

§03The anatomy of a quadratic

Every quadratic equation, when fully tidied up, can be written in this standard form:

ax² + bx + c = 0

Three letters — $\color{#ef8666}{a}$, $\color{#6fc2c2}{b}$, $\color{#e0b056}{c}$ — and one variable, $x$. The three letters are called coefficients. They are just numbers; they look like letters here because we want to talk about any quadratic, so we leave the numbers as placeholders.

Reading each piece

a THE LEADING COEFFICIENT

The number in front of $x^2$. It controls how "wide" or "narrow" the parabola is, and — most importantly — its sign decides whether the parabola opens upward (smile) or downward (frown).

If a > 0 → opens up.
If a < 0 → opens down.
If a = 0 → it's not a quadratic at all.

b THE MIDDLE TERM

The number in front of $x$ (just $x$, not $x^2$). It shifts the parabola sideways and up/down in combination with a. We mainly use it to find the vertex.

Can be positive, negative, or zero. If b = 0, the parabola sits with its vertex right on the y-axis.

c THE CONSTANT

The lone number with no $x$ attached. It tells you where the parabola crosses the y-axis — because at the y-axis, $x = 0$, so every term with $x$ disappears and only c is left.

The y-intercept is always the point $(0, \,$c$)$.

A concrete example

Suppose we have:

2x² + 5x − 3 = 0

Reading it: a = 2, b = 5, c = −3.

TRAP TO AVOID

The sign in front of a coefficient belongs to that coefficient. In the example above, the equation has $-3$ at the end, so c = −3, not $3$. Always carry the sign with the number. It saves a lot of grief later.

§04The parabola — what the graph looks like

A quadratic equation is something you can solve. But every quadratic also has a partner: a function, written

$y = $ a$x^2 + $ b$x + $ c

…which, when you plot it on a graph (every input $x$ paired with its output $y$), draws a parabola. The values of $x$ that make $y = 0$ — the spots where the curve crosses the x-axis — are exactly the solutions of the original equation. So the equation and the graph are the same thing told two different ways.

The parts of a parabola

Here is a labeled diagram of an upward-opening parabola:

Each part, in plain language

The vertex is the very tip of the parabola — the lowest point if the curve opens up, the highest point if it opens down. We label its coordinates $($h, k$)$ because $h$ and $k$ are traditional letters for "horizontal shift" and "vertical shift." The vertex is the most important point on a parabola; almost every problem hinges on finding it.

The axis of symmetry is the vertical line passing through the vertex. The parabola is a perfect mirror image across this line — fold the page along it and the two halves match exactly. Its equation is just $x = $ h (a vertical line at the vertex's x-coordinate).

The roots (also called zeros, solutions, or x-intercepts — same thing, four names) are the points where the parabola crosses the x-axis. At each root, $y = 0$, which is exactly the condition in the original equation a$x^2 + $ b$x + $ c $= 0$.

The y-intercept is where the parabola crosses the y-axis. Set $x = 0$ in the equation and every term with $x$ goes away, leaving just c. So the y-intercept is always $(0, \,$c$)$.

FOUR NAMES, ONE THING

"Root", "zero", "solution", and "x-intercept" all mean the same thing for a quadratic: a value of $x$ that makes $y = 0$. Don't let the vocabulary trick you into thinking there are four different concepts here. There is one concept and four teachers' favorite words for it.

§05Three forms, three lenses

The same quadratic can be written three different ways. None of them is "more correct" than the others — each one shows you something different at a glance. Switching between them is one of the main skills.

STANDARD FORM

$y = $ a$x^2 + $ b$x + $ c

The default. Easy to read off a, b, c. Tells you the y-intercept for free (it's c). This is the form you plug into the quadratic formula.

VERTEX FORM

$y = $ a$($x − h$)^2 +$ k

The form that hands you the vertex directly: it's at $($h, k$)$. Watch the sign — vertex form has a minus inside the parentheses, so $y = (x - 3)^2$ has vertex at $x = 3$, not $-3$.

FACTORED FORM

$y = $ a$($x − r₁$)($x − r₂$)$

The form that hands you the roots directly: r₁ and r₂ are the x-intercepts. Same sign rule — minus inside the parentheses, so the root really is positive r₁.

Same parabola, three voices

Watch the same curve get described three ways:

Standard

$y = x^2 - 4x + 3$

Vertex form

$y = (x - 2)^2 - 1$

→ Vertex at $($2, −1$)$.

Factored form

$y = (x - 1)(x - 3)$

→ Roots at $x = $1 and $x = $3.

Three identities, one parabola. If you multiplied out vertex form or factored form, you would land back at standard form every time. Try it: expand $(x-1)(x-3)$ and you get $x^2 - 4x + 3$. Expand $(x-2)^2 - 1$ and you get the same.

§06Roots, geometrically — solving is intersecting

Before we learn the mechanics of solving, let's pause and see what we're actually doing — visually. The roots aren't an abstract number puzzle. They're a specific point on the page.

The big idea

You've met two things that look related but separate: the parabola $y = $a$x^2 + $b$x + $c (a curve), and the equation a$x^2 + $b$x + $c $= 0$ (a thing you solve). The connection between them is the whole point of this section:

The geometric meaning of "solve" To solve a$x^2 + $b$x + $c $= 0$ is to find every $x$ where the parabola $y = $a$x^2 + $b$x + $c passes through the horizontal line $y = 0$.

The horizontal line $y = 0$ has a more familiar name: the x-axis. So the roots are exactly the x-values where the parabola crosses the x-axis. That's it. Every "method" we'll learn next — factoring, completing the square, the formula — is just a different way to compute those crossing points.

Why is a root a root?

Take the parabola $y = x^2 - 4$. It crosses the x-axis at $x = -2$ and $x = 2$ — you can see that in the picture below. Now plug $x = 2$ into the function: $y = (2)^2 - 4 = 4 - 4 = 0$. The function spits out $0$, which is exactly what we mean by "lying on the x-axis." Same for $x = -2$: $(-2)^2 - 4 = 0$. Being a root and being a point on the x-axis are literally the same property, just said two different ways.

The bigger principle

This idea is bigger than just quadratics, and worth absorbing because it'll come back forever in math. Any equation of the form $f(x) = g(x)$ is asking the same geometric question:

The universal translation "Solve $f(x) = g(x)$" $\;\;\Leftrightarrow\;\;$ "Find every $x$ where the graphs $y = f(x)$ and $y = g(x)$ intersect."

For our quadratic, the right-hand side is $g(x) = 0$, which draws the x-axis. But what if it were $g(x) = 5$ instead? Then we're asking where does the parabola cross the horizontal line $y = 5$? Those crossing points are the solutions of a$x^2 + $b$x + $c $= 5$.

Rearrange that equation: a$x^2 + $b$x + $c $- 5 = 0$, and you have a brand-new quadratic with the same shape as before but with c replaced by c$- 5$. The crossing points haven't moved (the parabola is in the same place, and the line is in the same place) — we just relabeled the question. Two ways to think about the same picture.

WORTH HIGHLIGHTING

Solving an equation is the same as finding where two graphs meet. Memorize that sentence. Every algebra problem from here to the end of high school can be re-cast through it. When you're stuck on an equation, sketch the graphs of both sides — sometimes the picture answers it before the algebra does.

§07How to solve a quadratic

"Solving" means: find the value(s) of $x$ that make the equation true — that is, the roots. There are four reliable methods, each best for a different situation. Know all four; reach for the one that fits the problem in front of you.

Method 1 — The square root method

Use this when there is no middle term (i.e., b = 0). The equation looks like a$x^2 + $ c $= 0$, and you can isolate $x^2$ and take the square root of both sides.

EXAMPLE

Solve $2x^2 - 18 = 0$.

  1. Move the constant
    $2x^2 = 18$
    Add 18 to both sides to get $x^2$ alone on one side.
  2. Isolate $x^2$
    $x^2 = 9$
    Divide both sides by 2.
  3. Take the square root
    $x = \pm 3$
    Both $+3$ and $-3$ square to $9$, so there are two solutions. The $\pm$ symbol (read "plus or minus") packages them together.

DON'T FORGET THE ±

Every positive number has two square roots: a positive one and a negative one. $3^2 = 9$ and $(-3)^2 = 9$. Writing only $x = 3$ is the most common mistake on these problems. Always write $\pm$.

Method 2 — Factoring

Factoring rewrites the quadratic as a product of two simpler pieces, then uses the zero product property: if two things multiplied together equal zero, at least one of them must be zero.

Zero product property If $\;A \cdot B = 0\;$ then $\;A = 0\;$ or $\;B = 0$ (or both).

EXAMPLE

Solve $x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0$.

  1. Find two numbers
    that multiply to 6 and add to −5
    These two numbers will become the roots (with a sign flip). Think: $-2$ and $-3$. Check: $(-2) \times (-3) = 6$ ✓ and $(-2) + (-3) = -5$ ✓.
  2. Write in factored form
    $(x - 2)(x - 3) = 0$
    The signs flip because vertex/factored form has a minus inside the parentheses. If the numbers were $-2$ and $-3$, the factors are $(x - 2)$ and $(x - 3)$.
  3. Apply zero product
    $x - 2 = 0\;$ or $\;x - 3 = 0$
    At least one factor has to be zero, so set each factor equal to zero separately.
  4. Solve each
    $x = 2\;$ or $\;x = 3$
    Two roots. Done.

WHEN FACTORING FAILS

Factoring is fast when the roots are whole numbers or nice fractions. When the roots are messy (irrational, or with weird decimals), no whole-number pair will work, and you should switch to the quadratic formula. You haven't done anything wrong — some quadratics just aren't factorable with nice numbers.

Method 3 — Completing the square

This method is less popular than factoring or the formula, but it's conceptually the most important — it's how the quadratic formula gets derived in the first place, and it's how you convert standard form into vertex form. Worth learning once, even if you don't use it daily.

The idea: take any quadratic and force it into the shape of a perfect square plus a leftover.

EXAMPLE

Solve $x^2 + 6x + 5 = 0$ by completing the square.

  1. Isolate the variable terms
    $x^2 + 6x = -5$
    Move the constant to the right side.
  2. Take half of b
    half of $6$ is $3$
    Take the coefficient of $x$ (the b), cut it in half. This is the magic number.
  3. Square that half
    $3^2 = 9$
    Square the half. This is what you'll add to both sides.
  4. Add to both sides
    $x^2 + 6x + 9 = -5 + 9$
    Add $9$ to both sides. Adding the same thing to both sides preserves the equality — but now the left side is a perfect square.
  5. Write the left as a square
    $(x + 3)^2 = 4$
    $x^2 + 6x + 9$ factors as $(x+3)(x+3) = (x+3)^2$. That "+3" inside is exactly the half from Step 2 — no coincidence; that's why the method works.
  6. Square root both sides
    $x + 3 = \pm 2$
    Remember the ±.
  7. Solve for $x$
    $x = -3 \pm 2$, so $x = -1$ or $x = -5$
    Two roots, as expected.

Method 4 — The quadratic formula

The universal method. It works on every quadratic, no matter how nasty. If completing the square is done abstractly on the generic equation a$x^2 + $ b$x + $ c $= 0$, this is what comes out:

$\;x = \dfrac{-\color{#6fc2c2}b \pm \sqrt{\color{#6fc2c2}b^{\,2} - 4\color{#ef8666}a\,\color{#e0b056}c}}{2\,\color{#ef8666}a}$

Three coefficients in, the roots come out. Memorize it. Sing it if you have to (there's actually a song to the tune of "Pop Goes the Weasel" — look it up).

EXAMPLE

Solve $2x^2 + 5x - 3 = 0$ with the formula.

  1. Identify the coefficients
    a = 2, b = 5, c = −3
    Always write these down before substituting. Carry the signs.
  2. Substitute into the formula
    $x = \dfrac{-(5) \pm \sqrt{(5)^2 - 4(2)(-3)}}{2(2)}$
    Each letter gets replaced by its number. Use parentheses around every substitution — this is the single best habit to avoid sign mistakes.
  3. Compute the discriminant (under the root)
    $(5)^2 - 4(2)(-3) = 25 + 24 = 49$
    $25 - (-24) = 25 + 24$. The two negatives multiply to a positive, so this whole thing ends up bigger, not smaller.
  4. Plug it back in
    $x = \dfrac{-5 \pm \sqrt{49}}{4} = \dfrac{-5 \pm 7}{4}$
    $\sqrt{49} = 7$.
  5. Split the ±
    $x = \dfrac{-5 + 7}{4} = \dfrac{2}{4} = \dfrac{1}{2}\;\;$ or $\;\;x = \dfrac{-5 - 7}{4} = \dfrac{-12}{4} = -3$
    Two roots: $x = \tfrac{1}{2}$ or $x = -3$.

WHICH METHOD WHEN?

No middle term? Square root method. Nice whole numbers and you can spot the factors? Factor it. Need vertex form? Complete the square. Anything else, or you're stuck? Quadratic formula always works.

§08The discriminant — predict before you solve

Look at the quadratic formula again. The piece under the square root sign has a name: the discriminant. It is usually denoted by the Greek letter $\Delta$ (capital delta).

$\Delta = \color{#6fc2c2}b^{\,2} - 4\color{#ef8666}a\,\color{#e0b056}c$

Why does anyone care about it on its own? Because the discriminant tells you, before you finish solving, how many real roots the quadratic has — and therefore where the parabola sits relative to the x-axis. Three cases:

IF Δ > 0

2roots

A positive number has two square roots, so you get $\sqrt{\Delta}$ as a real value, and $\pm\sqrt{\Delta}$ gives two different answers. The parabola crosses the x-axis at two distinct points.

IF Δ = 0

1root

$\sqrt{0} = 0$, so $\pm 0$ collapses both branches into the same answer. The parabola just barely touches the x-axis at one point — that touching point is also the vertex.

IF Δ < 0

0real roots

You can't take the square root of a negative number (with real numbers, anyway). The parabola never touches the x-axis — it floats entirely above it (if a>0) or entirely below it (if a<0).

So before doing any heavy lifting, compute $\Delta$. It costs you one multiplication and one subtraction, and tells you whether the rest of the work is even worth doing — or whether there are no real solutions to find.

SANITY CHECK BEFORE YOU FACTOR

If you suspect a quadratic factors nicely, check that $\Delta$ is a perfect square (like $1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49…$). If it is, the roots will be rational and factoring will work. If $\Delta$ is, say, $7$ or $20$, the roots will involve $\sqrt{7}$ or $\sqrt{20}$ — don't waste time hunting for whole-number factors; reach for the formula.

§09Finding the vertex without rewriting

If you have the parabola in vertex form, the vertex stares you in the face. But what if you only have standard form, and converting feels like a chore? There is a shortcut.

h $= \dfrac{-\color{#6fc2c2}b}{2\,\color{#ef8666}a}\;\;$ then $\;\;$k $= y$ at $x = $h

Two steps. The x-coordinate of the vertex is always $-$b$\,/\,(2\,$a$)$. The y-coordinate is then whatever the function spits out at that $x$. Plug in, evaluate, done.

EXAMPLE

Find the vertex of $y = 2x^2 - 8x + 1$.

  1. Identify a and b
    a = 2, b = −8
    We don't need c to find the vertex's x-coordinate.
  2. Compute h = −b / (2a)
    h $= \dfrac{-(-8)}{2(2)} = \dfrac{8}{4} = 2$
    Two negatives → positive in the numerator. So the vertex sits at $x = 2$.
  3. Compute k by plugging x = 2 into the equation
    k $= 2(2)^2 - 8(2) + 1$
    Substitute carefully. Always use parentheses.
  4. Simplify one piece at a time
    $= 2(4) - 16 + 1 = 8 - 16 + 1 = -7$
    $(2)^2 = 4$, then $2 \times 4 = 8$. Then $8 - 16 = -8$, then $-8 + 1 = -7$.
  5. Write the vertex as an ordered pair
    Vertex $= ($2, −7$)$
    Lowest point of the parabola (since a = 2 > 0 → opens up).

WHERE DOES −b/(2a) COME FROM?

It's not a magic formula. The parabola is symmetric, so the vertex sits exactly halfway between the two roots. The quadratic formula gives the roots as $\frac{-b \pm \sqrt{\Delta}}{2a}$. Average those two roots — the $\pm\sqrt{\Delta}$ parts cancel — and what's left is $\frac{-b}{2a}$. The vertex sits on the axis of symmetry, and that's where the axis of symmetry is.

§10How the numbers move and shape the curve

Vertex form is the friendliest for understanding what each parameter does to the picture. Start from the simplest possible parabola, $y = x^2$, which has vertex at the origin $(0, 0)$ and opens upward. Then watch what happens as we add ingredients.

Change a

$y = $a$x^2$ — large $|$a$|$ makes the parabola narrower (it grows fast as $x$ moves away from the vertex). Small $|$a$|$ makes it wider (it grows slowly). Flip the sign and the parabola flips upside down.

Change h

$y = (x - $h$)^2$ — shifts the parabola horizontally. Positive h moves it right; negative h moves it left. (Remember the minus sign inside the parentheses — it flips the sense of h.)

Change k

$y = x^2 + $k — shifts the parabola vertically. Positive k lifts it up; negative k lowers it. No sign trickery here — what you see is what you get.

Put all three together — $y = $a$(x - $h$)^2 + $k — and you can place the parabola anywhere, in any orientation, with any width. Three knobs, infinite parabolas.

THE ONE SIGN TRAP, AGAIN

$y = (x - 3)^2$ has its vertex at $x = +3$ (not $-3$). $y = (x + 3)^2$ has its vertex at $x = -3$ (not $+3$). The formula written in vertex form is $y = a(x - $h$)^2 + $k — with a minus in front of h. So whatever number you see being added or subtracted inside the parentheses, the vertex's x-coordinate is the opposite sign.

§11The width knob — pulling on a alone

You've seen all three coefficients tug on the parabola together in the main lab. Now let's freeze two of them and pull on just one — the leading coefficient a — to see how far the parabola can stretch and squash. At its limits, it stops looking like a parabola at all.

What a does, in isolation

Take the simplest possible quadratic: $y = $a$x^2$, with b $= 0$ and c $= 0$. The vertex sits at the origin, the axis of symmetry is the y-axis, and the only thing that can change about this parabola is its width — plus which way it opens. One knob, full control.

Bigger $|$a$|$ → the parabola climbs faster for the same step in $x$, so it looks narrower and steeper. Smaller $|$a$|$ → it climbs slower, so it looks wider and shallower. Flip the sign of a and the whole thing mirrors across the x-axis.

That sounds tame in a textbook. It's much more interesting at the boundaries — where the parabola starts approaching shapes it isn't supposed to be.

The two limits

Pulling a to its extremes reveals something important about what a parabola actually is.

|a| → ∞

The parabola pinches inward. As a gets larger and larger, the curve climbs so steeply that it's almost vertical right next to the y-axis. In the limit, the visible shape becomes a vertical spike — a near-line shooting up from the origin.

The parabola hasn't actually become a line — it's still a curve, with every point doing parabola-things. It just fits inside a thinner and thinner slice of the plane.

|a| → 0

The parabola flattens. As a shrinks toward zero, the curve climbs more and more slowly, until across the visible window it looks almost flat. At exactly a $= 0$, the equation becomes $y = 0$ — a horizontal line, the x-axis itself.

Here the parabola really does stop existing. The second-degree term vanishes and we're left with no curve at all.

WHY a = 0 IS BANNED, REVISITED

Way back in §02 we said "a can't be zero, or it's not a quadratic." Now you can see why visually. At a $= 0$, the $x^2$ term vanishes, the curve collapses into a straight horizontal line, and the whole machinery we've built — vertex, discriminant, axis of symmetry, two roots — stops applying. A horizontal line is a perfectly fine graph; it just isn't a parabola. The restriction isn't arbitrary, it's the math being honest about where its structure comes from.

THE NUMBER a IS A "STRETCH FACTOR"

Here's a useful way to think about a: for any point $(x, y)$ on the reference curve $y = x^2$, the corresponding point on $y = $a$x^2$ has the same $x$ but the y-coordinate multiplied by a. So a $= 3$ stretches every height by a factor of $3$ (the curve is $3$ times taller everywhere → looks narrower). a $= 0.25$ shrinks every height to a quarter (the curve is $4$ times shorter everywhere → looks wider). And a $= -1$ flips every height to its negative (upside-down mirror). The "width" change is really a height change — the parabola hasn't gotten wider, it's gotten shorter, so it looks like it spreads more.

§12Sideways & tilted — when parabolas turn

Every parabola we've drawn so far opens up or down. That's a consequence of writing equations as $y = f(x)$ — $y$ on the left, $x$ on the right. But there's no rule in nature saying a parabola must open vertically. Let's see what happens when we turn it.

The four cardinal directions

Watch what flips when we swap which variable is squared:

↑ OPENS UP

$y = \color{#ef8666}{a}x^2 + \color{#6fc2c2}{b}x + \color{#e0b056}{c}$

$x$ is squared, $y$ is alone. Axis of symmetry: vertical. Opens up when a > 0.

↓ OPENS DOWN

$y = \color{#ef8666}{a}x^2 + \color{#6fc2c2}{b}x + \color{#e0b056}{c}$

Same equation, but with a < 0. The negative leading coefficient flips it upside down.

→ OPENS RIGHT

$x = \color{#ef8666}{a}y^2 + \color{#6fc2c2}{b}y + \color{#e0b056}{c}$

Now $y$ is squared and $x$ is alone. Axis of symmetry: horizontal. Opens right when a > 0.

← OPENS LEFT

$x = \color{#ef8666}{a}y^2 + \color{#6fc2c2}{b}y + \color{#e0b056}{c}$

Same as opens right, but with a < 0.

The pattern is simple: whichever variable is squared decides the orientation.

$x$ squared → parabola opens vertically (up or down). $y$ squared → parabola opens horizontally (right or left). The sign of the leading coefficient picks which of the two directions it is.

A picture of all four

Sideways parabolas aren't functions of x

A sideways parabola like $x = y^2 + 2y - 3$ is a perfectly legitimate curve, but it isn't a function of $x$. If you draw a vertical line anywhere through it, the line will generally cross the curve twice — meaning there are two y-values for the same x-value. This is the vertical line test failing.

But the curve is a function of $y$: every $y$-value produces exactly one $x$-value. So it's a function — just one with the input on the y-axis instead of the x-axis. The rest of the anatomy (vertex, axis of symmetry, roots, intercepts) works exactly the same, just rotated $90°$.

THE "ROOTS" CHANGE ADDRESS

For an up-or-down parabola, "roots" mean where the curve crosses the x-axis (because that's where $y = 0$). For a sideways parabola, the natural analog is where the curve crosses the y-axis — where $x = 0$. Same concept, but the axis you read off changes.

At an arbitrary angle

What if the parabola is tilted at $30°$, or $45°$, or any angle that isn't a multiple of $90°$? Then neither $x^2$ alone nor $y^2$ alone is enough. The equation needs a new ingredient — a cross-term with both variables multiplied together: $xy$.

General conic form (handles any rotation) $\color{#ef8666}{A}x^2 + \color{#d978c8}{B}\,xy + \color{#6fc2c2}{C}y^2 + \color{#e0b056}{D}x + \color{#e0b056}{E}y + F = 0$

This is the general equation for any conic section — parabola, ellipse, hyperbola, or circle — in any orientation. The key new coefficient is $\color{#d978c8}{B}$, the number in front of the $xy$ term:

When $\color{#d978c8}{B} = 0$, the curve is aligned with the axes (opens straight up, down, left, or right). When $\color{#d978c8}{B} \neq 0$, the curve is tilted. The bigger $|\color{#d978c8}{B}|$ is, the more rotation the equation is encoding.

WHY THE xy TERM CAUSES ROTATION

Without going into the linear algebra: if you take a "standard" upright parabola $y = x^2$ and rotate the whole coordinate plane by an angle $\theta$, then substitute the rotated $x$ and $y$ back in, you'll get an expression with $\cos(\theta)$ and $\sin(\theta)$ multiplied into all the terms. The new cross-term that appears is proportional to $\sin(\theta)\cos(\theta)$ — which is zero only when $\theta = 0°, 90°, 180°,$ or $270°$. For any other angle, a non-zero $xy$ term shows up. So the rotation literally creates the cross-term out of the original equation.

YOU WON'T BE TESTED ON ROTATED CONICS (USUALLY)

At the high-school level, you'll almost always work with parabolas that open up, down, left, or right — never tilted. But knowing that tilted parabolas exist, and that the $xy$ term is what makes them tilt, demystifies a lot of weirder equations you might see later in calculus or physics. The big takeaway: the form of the equation tells you the orientation of the curve, and there are no exceptions.

§14Three worked examples, end to end

Each example below is solved fully, with every step labeled. The goal is not for you to memorize them — the goal is for the pattern to become obvious after the third one.

Example 1 — Factor it

Solve $x^2 + 4x - 12 = 0$ and sketch its parabola.

  1. Identify a, b, c
    a = 1, b = 4, c = −12
    When there's no number in front of $x^2$, a = 1.
  2. Check the discriminant for sanity
    $\Delta = (4)^2 - 4(1)(-12) = 16 + 48 = 64$
    $64$ is a perfect square ($8^2$), so factoring will work cleanly.
  3. Find two numbers
    multiplying to −12 and adding to 4
    Try pairs: $(6, -2)$ → product $-12$ ✓, sum $4$ ✓. Done.
  4. Factored form
    $(x + 6)(x - 2) = 0$
    Signs flip from the numbers found above.
  5. Roots
    $x = -6\;$ or $\;x = 2$
    Zero product: each factor in turn = 0.
  6. Vertex x-coordinate
    h $= \dfrac{-(4)}{2(1)} = -2$
    Halfway between the two roots ($-6$ and $2$), as expected.
  7. Vertex y-coordinate
    k $= (-2)^2 + 4(-2) - 12 = 4 - 8 - 12 = -16$
    Plug $x = -2$ back into the original equation.
  8. Summary
    Vertex $($−2, −16$)$, roots $x = $−6, 2, y-intercept $(0, $−12$)$, opens up.
    Everything you need to sketch the parabola.

Example 2 — Use the formula

Solve $3x^2 - x - 5 = 0$.

  1. Identify a, b, c
    a = 3, b = −1, c = −5
    The middle term $-x$ means b = −1, not $-x$ itself.
  2. Discriminant
    $\Delta = (-1)^2 - 4(3)(-5) = 1 + 60 = 61$
    $61$ is not a perfect square — so the roots will be irrational. Don't try to factor; use the formula.
  3. Apply the formula
    $x = \dfrac{-(-1) \pm \sqrt{61}}{2(3)} = \dfrac{1 \pm \sqrt{61}}{6}$
    Two negatives in the numerator becoming positive: $-(-1) = 1$.
  4. The two roots
    $x \approx \dfrac{1 + 7.81}{6} \approx 1.47\;\;$ or $\;\;x \approx \dfrac{1 - 7.81}{6} \approx -1.13$
    $\sqrt{61} \approx 7.81$. Decimal approximations are perfectly fine when the exact form is ugly.

Example 3 — Complete the square to get vertex form

Rewrite $y = x^2 - 6x + 11$ in vertex form, then state the vertex.

  1. Group the x-terms
    $y = (x^2 - 6x) + 11$
    Pull the constant outside; we'll deal with it separately.
  2. Take half of b, square it
    half of $-6$ is $-3$, and $(-3)^2 = 9$
    This is the number that will complete the square.
  3. Add and subtract that number inside
    $y = (x^2 - 6x + 9 - 9) + 11$
    We add $9$ to create the square, then subtract $9$ to keep the equation unchanged. This is the key move — adding zero in disguise.
  4. Factor the perfect square, combine constants
    $y = (x - 3)^2 + 2$
    $x^2 - 6x + 9 = (x - 3)^2$, and $-9 + 11 = 2$. Done.
  5. Read off the vertex
    Vertex $= ($3, 2$)$
    Remember the sign trap: $(x - 3)^2$ gives vertex x-coordinate $+3$, not $-3$.

NOTICE THE PATTERN

Every quadratic problem reduces to: identify a, b, c → check the discriminant → pick a method → carry the signs carefully → solve. That's the whole loop. Once it becomes automatic, these problems stop feeling like puzzles and start feeling like a recipe.

§15Practice — try, then peek

Each problem below has a hidden solution. Try it first; then tap the button to compare. Don't worry if your work looks different — sometimes there are multiple valid paths.

1. Solve $x^2 - 7x + 10 = 0$ by factoring.
Show solution

Find two numbers that multiply to 10 and add to −7: that's $-2$ and $-5$.

$(x - 2)(x - 5) = 0$, so $x = 2$ or $x = 5$.

2. Use the quadratic formula to solve $2x^2 + 7x + 3 = 0$.
Show solution

a = 2, b = 7, c = 3.

$\Delta = 7^2 - 4(2)(3) = 49 - 24 = 25$.

$x = \dfrac{-7 \pm \sqrt{25}}{4} = \dfrac{-7 \pm 5}{4}$.

So $x = -\tfrac{1}{2}$ or $x = -3$.

3. Find the vertex of $y = -x^2 + 4x + 1$.
Show solution

a = −1, b = 4.

h $= \dfrac{-(4)}{2(-1)} = \dfrac{-4}{-2} = 2$.

k $= -(2)^2 + 4(2) + 1 = -4 + 8 + 1 = 5$.

Vertex: $($2, 5$)$. Since a < 0, this is the maximum point.

4. How many real roots does $x^2 - 2x + 5 = 0$ have?
Show solution

Compute the discriminant: $\Delta = (-2)^2 - 4(1)(5) = 4 - 20 = -16$.

Since $\Delta < 0$, there are no real roots. The parabola floats entirely above the x-axis.

5. Convert $y = x^2 + 8x + 10$ to vertex form.
Show solution

Half of $8$ is $4$, and $4^2 = 16$.

$y = (x^2 + 8x + 16) - 16 + 10$

$y = (x + 4)^2 - 6$.

Vertex: $($−4, −6$)$.

6. A ball thrown straight up has height $h(t) = -5t^2 + 20t + 1$ (meters, $t$ in seconds). When does it reach maximum height? What is the maximum height?
Show solution

This is asking for the vertex. a = −5, b = 20.

$t_{\text{vertex}} = \dfrac{-(20)}{2(-5)} = \dfrac{-20}{-10} = 2$ seconds.

$h(2) = -5(2)^2 + 20(2) + 1 = -20 + 40 + 1 = 21$ meters.

The ball reaches a maximum height of $21$ m at $t = 2$ s.

§16Where these show up in real life

PROJECTILE MOTION

Anything thrown, kicked, launched, or fired through the air follows a parabolic path (ignoring air resistance). The vertex of that parabola is the moment of peak height; the roots are when it left the ground and when it lands. The quadratic formula gives you the time of impact.

SATELLITE DISHES & HEADLIGHTS

The cross-section of a parabolic dish or reflector is — unsurprisingly — a parabola. Parabolas have a magical geometric property: every ray parallel to the axis bounces off the inside and converges to a single point (the focus). Headlights run this backward: put a bulb at the focus and the dish spits out parallel beams.

OPTIMIZATION

If profit, area, distance, or time depends on a quadratic expression, finding the vertex tells you the maximum or minimum value. "How wide should I cut the fence so the rectangular area is largest?" — that's a parabola vertex problem.

BRIDGES & ARCHES

The cables of a suspension bridge hang in a curve very close to a parabola when the load is evenly distributed across the deck. Many decorative arches are designed as parabolas because they distribute weight efficiently.

SPINNING LIQUIDS

Spin a bucket of water steadily — the surface of the water forms a parabola. Astronomers use this to grind giant telescope mirrors: spin liquid mercury, let it harden, you've got a parabolic mirror with no machining required.

PHYSICS — KINETIC ENERGY

The kinetic energy of a moving object is $\tfrac{1}{2}mv^2$ — a quadratic in $v$. That's why a car going twice as fast takes four times the distance to stop, not twice. The parabola is right there.

Quick reference

Standard form

$y = $a$x^2 + $b$x + $c

Vertex form

$y = $a$(x - $h$)^2 + $k$\;$ → vertex at $($h, k$)$

Factored form

$y = $a$(x - $r₁$)(x - $r₂$)\;$ → roots at r₁, r₂

Quadratic formula

$x = \dfrac{-\color{#6fc2c2}b \pm \sqrt{\color{#6fc2c2}b^{\,2} - 4\color{#ef8666}a\,\color{#e0b056}c}}{2\,\color{#ef8666}a}$

Discriminant

$\Delta = $b$^2 - 4$ac
$\Delta > 0$ → 2 real roots
$\Delta = 0$ → 1 real root (the vertex)
$\Delta < 0$ → 0 real roots

Vertex shortcut

h $= \dfrac{-\color{#6fc2c2}b}{2\,\color{#ef8666}a}$
k $= $ plug h into the equation

Opens up or down?

a > 0 → opens up (vertex is minimum)
a < 0 → opens down (vertex is maximum)

y-intercept

Always $(0, \,$c$)$ — the constant term, period.

Zero product property

If $A \cdot B = 0$, then $A = 0$ or $B = 0$ (or both). This is what makes factoring work.

FINAL ADVICE

Don't trust your head with arithmetic in these problems — write every step on the page. Use parentheses around every substitution. Always carry the sign with the coefficient. And when in doubt, plug the answer back in and check that it actually satisfies the equation. That last habit will save you on every test for the rest of your life.
